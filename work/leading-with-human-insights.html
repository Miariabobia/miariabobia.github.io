Case Study

What I Found in the Stores Changed Everything

I walked into T-Mobile stores across Minnesota expecting to find frustrated employees struggling with broken tools. What I found instead was passion. Frontline employees who genuinely loved their work, who thrived on helping customers, who mentored each other with the care of a sports team. The broken systems weren't just inefficient — they were getting in the way of people who wanted to do something meaningful. That realization didn't just shape this project. It changed how I think about design.

The Challenge

T-Mobile wanted to become a Digital First company, with goals of reducing calls to care centers by 75% and retail store visits by 50%, by 2026. But there was a problem: no one had shared clarity on what that actually meant for the people closest to the customer — frontline retail and care employees. Leadership had a goal. The frontline had the reality. The gap between them was wide and unmapped.

A cross-functional tiger team was assembled to close that gap.

How might we empower Retail reps with the tools to serve customers with the least effort?

This wasn't a product brief. It was a discovery mandate. Before anyone could design a solution, we needed to understand what was actually happening in those stores — and why.

The Team

This wasn't a small research exercise. It was a nationally coordinated effort with a $10K field research budget and a team assembled specifically for this challenge. I was one of two designers selected, embedded alongside researchers, a systems architect, an AI/ML lead from leadership, product managers, and a sprint master.

The geographic scope was intentional. We didn't want to study one market and generalize. We needed to understand variation — urban vs. rural, high-traffic vs. quiet, diverse regional demographics. We covered Minneapolis, Los Angeles, New York, Atlanta, Albuquerque, Las Vegas, Philadelphia, Kansas City, and Austin.

Going Into the Field

I was stationed in Minneapolis. The AI/ML lead from our leadership team flew out specifically to conduct field research alongside me — which told me something important about how seriously the org was taking this work.

We didn't sit in a conference room and run interviews. We went into stores. We shadowed experts through full shifts. We watched how they moved, how they navigated between systems, how they handled customers when something went wrong. We went from urban Minneapolis to small towns hours away — deliberately choosing variation so we could see what changed and what stayed the same across contexts. At the end of each day, we asked them all the same question -- "If you had a magic wand, what would you change and why?".

What I didn't expect was what I found underneath the operational problems. These employees weren't just doing a job. They were proud of it. They competed with each other — in a healthy, energizing way — to close deals, to solve customer problems, to hit metrics that mattered to them personally. They mentored each other. The senior reps invested in the junior ones. The culture was alive in a way the digital experience had completely failed to reflect.

Three Insights That Changed the Direction

1

Fragmentation was the enemy of flow

Frontline employees were context-switching across multiple systems just to serve a single customer. Every extra tap, every app switch, every moment of searching for information was a moment stolen from the customer interaction they actually cared about. The friction wasn't just inefficient — it was demoralizing. They were passionate about serving customers. The tools were getting in their way.

Multi-system navigation Knowledge silos Manual workarounds Interrupted customer interactions
2

Frontline employees operate like athletes

This wasn't a workforce going through the motions. They were competitive, performance-driven, and deeply team-oriented. They tracked their metrics. They celebrated wins. They held each other accountable. The culture looked more like a sports team than a retail floor — and the tools they were given had completely failed to reflect that energy. A tool designed for passive task completion was being used by people who thrived on momentum and mastery.

Performance culture Team dynamics Intrinsic motivation Competitive drive
3

Growth and mentorship were core to their identity

The best frontline employees didn't just want to do their job well — they wanted to level up, and to bring others with them. Senior reps invested in junior ones. Knowledge transfer happened informally, constantly, and with genuine care. But there was no infrastructure to support it. The mentorship culture they had built for themselves was invisible to the systems designed to support them.

Peer mentorship Knowledge transfer Growth orientation Invisible culture

Synthesis: From Observations to Shared Truth

Field research generates noise before it generates signal. Across 8+ cities and dozens of store visits, we came back with an overwhelming volume of observations. The synthesis work — turning that into something the team could act on — was as important as the research itself.

We ran affinity mapping sessions to cluster observations into themes, built three distinct personas grounded in the real people we'd met, and developed a problem tree that connected surface-level pain points to their systemic root causes. The goal wasn't a report. It was a shared understanding that the whole team — designers, researchers, product managers, engineers — could build from.

The pain points clustered into five clear themes: system fragmentation and speed, information availability and accuracy, unmet customer expectations, financial transaction complexity, and the invisible cost of cross-channel communication gaps. These weren't abstract findings. They had faces — the employees we'd spent days with in Minneapolis and LA and New York.

Looking Outside T-Mobile

T-Mobile had won JD Power awards for customer service multiple years in a row. I didn't think that meant we had all the answers.

I benchmarked against companies known for exceptional frontline service — particularly American Express, which had built a model that leaned directly into the competitive, fulfillment-driven nature of their representatives. Their approach wasn't about enforcement or incentive structures. It was about designing an environment where people found genuine meaning in the work. The result was exceptional service — not as a policy, but as a culture.

I saw the same potential in T-Mobile's frontline employees. They already had the passion. They already had the competitive drive. What they were missing was a tool designed around how they actually worked.

The question wasn't how to make frontline employees work harder. It was how to stop the tools from getting in the way of people who already wanted to.

The Vision That Emerged

The field research gave us something more valuable than a list of pain points. It gave us a design philosophy.

I began advocating for a reimagined Support Hub — not a better interface layered on top of broken systems, but a native, contextual, personalized platform designed from the ground up for the way frontline employees actually thought and worked. One that surfaced the right information at the right moment. That reflected their performance culture. That supported the mentorship relationships they'd built for themselves. That was connected on the backend to the systems they needed — without requiring them to navigate those systems manually.

◆

The Design Principles

Contextual: Surface what's needed for this customer, this interaction, this moment — not everything all at once.

Personal: Reflect the individual employee's performance, growth, and team context.

Connected: Tied directly to backend systems so employees don't have to be the connective tissue between fragmented tools.

Human: Designed to amplify what frontline employees were already trying to do — not to replace their judgment with automation.

What Happened After

I rolled off the tiger team and returned to my team on Service. The broader sprint work continued without me — and the foundation we built went on to influence the formation of what became T-Mobile's Assisted team, a dedicated unit focused on frontline experiences.

On my own team, I was tasked with designing a temporary support solution in the existing T-Mobile app. With everything I'd learned in the field, I couldn't design something temporary and call it done. I advocated for it to be more.

It became more. Support was elevated from a stopgap feature to a permanent fixture in the main navigation — alongside Shop, Home, and Manage. I delivered V1 as a foundation, and began deep discovery for V2: the truly reimagined version, grounded in everything the tiger team had uncovered.

V2 was scrapped when the app project was replaced by T-Life. Some of the vision made its way into T-Life's manage experience through another team. I recognized it wasn't a battle worth fighting at that point — the right move was to protect the relationship and redirect energy toward where I could still build.

What I Took Away

The most important thing I learned in those stores wasn't about systems or workflows. It was about what happens when technology fails the people it's supposed to serve. Frontline employees weren't burned out because the work was hard. They were frustrated because the tools made meaningful work harder than it needed to be. That's a design failure — and it's a solvable one.

I also learned that the best insights don't come from asking people what they need. They come from watching what they do when no one is asking. The competitive culture, the mentorship dynamics, the pride in performance — none of that would have surfaced in a survey. It required presence.

That principle has shaped every discovery engagement I've done since. Before I design anything, I want to understand what drives the person who will use it. Not just their pain points. Their motivations. Their identity. The thing that makes them care about doing the work well.

Because when you design for that — when you build tools that honor the passion people already bring — the product stops being something they have to use and becomes something they actually want to.
