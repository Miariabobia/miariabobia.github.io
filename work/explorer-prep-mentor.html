Building a Design Pipeline: Mentoring the Next Generation of UX Practitioners

T-Mobile's Explorer Prep Career Launch Program offered students a non-traditional path into tech — skipping the four-year degree in favor of a paid internship, college credit, and a direct line to employment. I was a mentor for the UX/UI design track. What followed went further than I expected.

Context

The Edmonds School District serves over 20,000 students across south Snohomish County — a majority-minority district where 55% of students are from underrepresented communities and nearly 31% qualify for free or reduced lunch. For many students here, a four-year university isn't a realistic path. The Explorer Prep program was designed with that reality in mind.

The model was straightforward but meaningful: high school juniors from Edmonds School District would complete a paid summer internship at T-Mobile, take Running Start classes at Edmonds College during their senior year, earn a certificate in Full Stack Development, Cybersecurity, or UX/UI Design — and receive a guaranteed interview upon completion. Real work, real credit, real opportunity.

This wasn't a simulation or a classroom exercise. These students were sitting in real meetings, working on real problems, alongside people who do this for a living.

My Approach

My mentees came in with backgrounds in arts and fine arts — creative instincts, but no framework for translating that into product thinking. My job was to build the bridge.

Live Immersion

Students joined my actual meetings — stakeholder reviews, critique sessions, working sessions. No simulated environments. They saw design work in its messy, real form: ambiguity, tradeoffs, feedback.

Design Thinking Curriculum

I built exercises grounded in design thinking fundamentals — problem framing, user empathy, ideation, and critique — tailored to students who had never worked in a professional product environment.

Independent Project Work

Between sessions, students worked on assigned projects independently, developing the self-direction and time management that a professional role requires.

Professional Identity

I took their professional headshots and helped them build their LinkedIn profiles. For students entering the workforce without a degree, how they present themselves matters early.

Year Two — From Mentee to Collaborator

One student stood out across both years of the program. In year two, I brought her into active work on Magenta A11y — a redesign of T-Mobile's accessibility resource website.

She didn't observe. She contributed. Her work focused on discovery research and information architecture drafting — foundational work that shaped how the site was ultimately structured. For a student still completing her certificate program, this was the real thing.

What I Took Away

Mentoring these students changed how I think about design education. Most design programs assume a certain starting point — exposure to tools, familiarity with critique, some understanding of what a product career looks like. These students had none of that. What they had was creative instinct, hunger, and a willingness to be uncomfortable.

Teaching someone with no frame of reference forces you to articulate things you've long taken for granted. Why do we frame problems before jumping to solutions? What does good feedback actually sound like? What does it mean to advocate for a user you've never met? I became a sharper practitioner by having to explain these things clearly.

The 75% conversion rate is meaningful. But what I think about more is what it took to get there — and what it means that three people who might never have found their way into this field now have careers in it.
