Designing a Learning System Inside Ambiguity

Explorer Prep had no curriculum. Students arrived on day one with high uncertainty, no shared framework, and no clear sense of what they were expected to produce. My job wasn't just to mentor — it was to design the conditions for learning inside ambiguity. That's a systems design problem.

The Design Problem

T-Mobile's Explorer Prep Career Launch Program was built around a meaningful idea: partner with Edmonds College and the Edmonds School District to give high school students a non-traditional path into tech — paid, accredited, and connected directly to employment. No four-year degree required.

The Edmonds School District serves a majority-minority community where 55% of students are from underrepresented groups and nearly 31% qualify for free or reduced lunch. For many of these students, a traditional university path isn't accessible. This program was designed with that reality in mind.

But when students arrived for the UX track, there was no curriculum waiting for them. No structured arc. No shared expectations. Just time, ambiguity, and an 8-week window with real career stakes at the end.

Without structure, the risk was clear: time would be consumed by uncertainty rather than growth. Confidence would erode before it could form.

I saw that risk immediately. The constraints were real:

No established curriculum

No clear expectations for students

Students entering with high uncertainty

Compressed 8-week timeline

Real-world career stakes

Cross-functional exposure required

Three Strategic Interventions

1

Set the psychological foundation on day one

I led the opening session — not because I had to, but because day one establishes everything: psychological safety, shared norms, permission to not know things yet. Students needed to feel oriented before they could learn.

The icebreaker wasn't filler. It was organizational design. I was shaping the environment before any content was introduced.

Psychological safety Norm-setting Environmental design
2

Designed a structured learning arc from scratch

I built a curriculum because there wasn't one. My mentees came in with backgrounds in arts and fine arts — creative instincts without a professional framework. My job was to build the bridge between what they already knew and what product thinking actually requires.

The arc was sequenced intentionally: start with problem framing, build toward user empathy, introduce critique, then move to independent project work with increasing autonomy. Structure wasn't meant to constrain exploration — it was meant to channel it productively.

Sequenced learning arc Design thinking fundamentals Critique frameworks Progressive autonomy Decision-framing exercises
3

Integrated students into real work — not simulations

Students joined my actual meetings: stakeholder reviews, critique sessions, working sessions. They saw design work in its real form — ambiguous, pressured, and full of tradeoffs. No artificial exercises standing in for the real thing.

I also invested in their professional identity: took their headshots, helped them build LinkedIn profiles. For students entering the workforce without a degree, how they present themselves is a meaningful part of their competitive positioning.

Live stakeholder exposure Real project immersion Professional identity LinkedIn & headshots

Year Two — From Mentee to Collaborator

One student stood out across both years of the program. In year two, I brought her into active work on Magenta A11y — a redesign of T-Mobile's accessibility resource website.

She didn't observe. She contributed. Her work focused on discovery research and information architecture drafting — foundational work that shaped how the site was ultimately structured. For a student still completing her certificate program, this was the real thing.

The Magenta A11y project won a Gaady's Award, recognizing excellence in accessible and inclusive design. My mentee's discovery work and information architecture contributions were part of the work that earned that recognition.

What I Took Away

Mentorship is systems design at a human scale. The constraints are the same: ambiguous inputs, unclear success criteria, high stakes, compressed timelines. The output isn't a screen or a flow — it's a person who can navigate complexity independently.

Designing structure doesn't eliminate ambiguity. It channels it productively. That's what the curriculum did. That's what day one did. That's what integrating students into real work did — it gave them a container to hold the uncertainty while they built the capability to work through it.

There's something deeper here too. Ambiguity, when left unaddressed, is isolating. Students who don't know what's expected of them don't just waste time — they disappear into uncertainty alone. I've seen what that does to confidence. What I designed was structure, yes — but underneath that, it was a counter to that specific kind of loneliness.

The 75% conversion rate is the metric. But what I carry forward is the pattern: when you design the right conditions for learning, people exceed what anyone expected of them — including themselves.
