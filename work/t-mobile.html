Account Hub Shop Redesign: Leading Through Crisis

Role Lead Product Designer (Account Hub)
Timeline Initial 1-week handoff window; expanded to 4 weeks after cross-team reprioritization.
Impact Delivered under shifting priorities; established scalable foundation
The Challenge

Inherited a destabilized enterprise commerce redesign amid shifting priorities and unclear scope.

The Action

Audited 8 flows, removed exploratory scope creep, defined a foundational MVP (3 flows), established a single source of truth operating model, and aligned cross-functional partners during roadmap volatility.

The Result

Delivered research-validated designs on time; established scalable foundation for Q1 launch.

Overview

One week before engineering handoff, the lead designer left the project. I was leading Account Hub under a Principal Designer, overseeing scope and strategy across multiple teams. When Shop destabilized, I stepped in. I was the only designer with cross-domain visibility across Hub, prior e-commerce experience, and established executive trust — ramping someone new would have introduced risk we couldn’t absorb.

What followed wasn't a one-week sprint. It was four weeks of diagnosis, unglamorous audit work, and proactive definition — most of it done without full team support — that made execution possible when Shop suddenly became the company's top priority with a week to deliver.

The Work Nobody Saw

Very quickly, IT realized the original timeline was unrealistic. Shop was deprioritized to #3 while engineering redirected to other initiatives. We gained time — but lost active support.

I audited all 8 flows, mapping dependencies and completion states. I met directly with cross-functional partners to surface real requirements — separating what was essential from what had accumulated through scope creep. I audited the live experience to identify structural gaps, friction points, and enterprise edge cases the previous designs hadn't accounted for. I removed exploratory features that didn't serve the foundational experience and made targeted UI and flow changes grounded in what the current experience actually needed.

This was slow, unglamorous work done largely without support. But it produced something essential: clarity about what Shop actually needed to be — and prevented exploratory ambiguity from reaching development when Shop reactivated.

To structure decision-making going forward, I created an experience review document — inspired by Amazon's review process — that gave the team a shared operating system for saying no. It captured stakeholder mapping, scope boundaries, requirements provenance, escalation paths, and aligned decisions. When new requests surfaced, we asked: does this serve the defined success criteria? When scope pressure returned, we referenced documented alignment instead of relitigating. The framework became a reusable template across Hub initiatives and is still used today.

When Priorities Shifted

Two weeks in, Shop jumped from #3 to #1. The preparation work I'd done in the quiet period was now the foundation the team needed to execute quickly.

With alignment in place, I proposed scoping to 3 foundational flows:

01

Add a Line

Highest-frequency, highest revenue-impact growth action.

02

Non-Core Devices (IoT/Dialpad)

Significant B2B hardware transactions.

03

Lower Funnel (Checkout)

Shared completion layer across all flows.

Together, these created a complete purchase journey and a scalable architecture future flows could plug into.

I deprioritized 5 flows — including Saved Orders, a committed Q1 feature. Rather than framing these as cuts, I reframed around protection: to protect architectural integrity, to protect launch confidence, to protect the ability to deliver deferred features well.I advocated for a fast-follow release on Saved Orders (4–6 weeks) rather than risk a compromised launch. Stakeholders aligned.

The Pivot

Days before the VP review, the design direction shifted while I was briefly out. The revised concept prioritized long-term scalability but diverged from established commerce patterns.

The VP's feedback was direct: "Don't reinvent the wheel."

In critical workflows like checkout, users don't want novelty. They want clarity and predictability.

I pivoted to conventional interaction patterns — not as a creative compromise, but as a strategic choice. Working with the team, I delivered the revised designs on time.

Systemic Impact

While executing, I collaborated with our Design Systems lead to build reusable components adopted by other vertical leads across the platform. The operating framework and component patterns reduced duplication, improved consistency, and accelerated subsequent vertical work. We weren't just solving for Shop — we were building infrastructure for the broader Hub redesign.

The Outcome

We delivered on time. The three foundational flows are ready for launch at the end of Q1, with quality intact and a stable architecture that future flows can plug into without redesign.

While the flows aren't live yet, pre-launch research validated the direction. Testing confirmed improved task clarity, reduced friction, and stronger confidence among business users. Deferred flows are sequenced intentionally on the roadmap with clear rationale.

Moving Forward

With the foundation stabilized, we shifted from crisis management to proactive iteration. I'm working with research partners to identify high-impact opportunities grounded in usage data and customer behavior, and facilitating cross-functional prioritization to sequence iterative improvements alongside 2026 roadmap initiatives. The shift is intentional: from reactive scope management to evidence-based platform evolution.

What I Learned

Clarity is the most valuable artifact in chaos. When priorities are ambiguous, the first deliverable isn't screens — it's definition.

Hard calls are easier when anchored to principle. Frameworks turn personal pushback into shared decision-making.

Senior work isn't about having more answers. It's about structuring the path forward when none exists — including when no one is watching.

Shipping less with integrity builds trust — especially when convention is chosen intentionally in critical workflows.

This project also surfaced a structural gap worth naming: design was brought in at the end of an IT-driven process, limiting our ability to influence technical decisions that directly affected the experience. Going forward, I'm advocating for earlier design involvement in infrastructure planning — not to own the roadmap, but to ensure experience implications are considered before constraints are locked.
